Patient Education

Correcting cloudiness: understanding cataracts

A cataract is the change in clarity, or clouding, of the lens within the eye. About half of Americans aged 65–74 develop some cataract, and over 90% of cases come simply from natural aging.

Signs you may notice

Early on, it can feel "as if one is looking through a dirty window."

  • Blurred or softened sharp images
  • Dulled or faded light colors
  • Increasing difficulty seeing at night
  • Reading glasses or bifocals no longer help

Lowering your risk

There's no proven non-surgical cure yet, but you can protect your eyes:

  • A diet rich in antioxidants (vitamin A, carotene)
  • UV-protective glasses and lenses
  • Quitting smoking (smokers face up to twice the risk)
  • Regular exams to track changes early

What cataract surgery looks like

An outpatient procedure performed under operating-microscope magnification. The entire procedure typically takes about 15 minutes, and once a cataract is removed, it cannot return.

01

Gentle anesthesia

Topical anesthesia without needles, plus light IV sedation for comfort.

02

Micro-incision

An incision of 2.8 mm or less allows ultrasonic phacoemulsification of the lens.

03

Lens removal

The cloudy lens material is removed while the clear lens capsule is preserved.

04

New lens implant

A foldable lens is inserted through the same incision and permanently replaces the old one.

Recovery is quick. Most patients feel minimal discomfort, wear a light protective shield, and return to normal activities within days. Vision improves soon after surgery and keeps getting better over several weeks.
